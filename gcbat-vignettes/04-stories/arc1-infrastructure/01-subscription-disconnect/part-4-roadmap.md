## Part 4 — Roadmap & Futures

### 24-Month Implementation Timeline

GC-BAT's framework was not just analysis. It was an action plan with dates, responsibilities, metrics.

#### Q2 2025 — Foundation Phase

**Government action.** Parliament debates the Essential Services Continuity Act in the first week of April. Priya Sharma's bill, incorporating GC-BAT technical standards. Expected bipartisan passage after the AllConnex crisis. Implementation timeline: 90 days post-passage for regulatory authority establishment. By week four, ACCC launches formal investigation into AllConnex — consumer protection violations, misleading conduct, unconscionable conduct. Timeline: six-month investigation. Potential penalties: $50 million-plus fines, structural remedies, executive accountability. By week eight, Technology Safety Commission is established as an independent regulatory body, arms-length from industry. Funding: $120 million annually from a tech industry levy — 0.5% of annual revenue for companies above $100 million. Powers: audit authority, penalty enforcement, emergency intervention. First Chair: Dr. Linda Cheng, former ACCC Commissioner. By week twelve, the National Digital Resilience Campaign launches — $40 million over two years. Content: how to operate your technology manually, understanding your digital rights, community resilience planning. Multilingual, across TV, radio, online, community workshops. Target: 70% household participation in basic resilience training.

**Standards action.** Standards Australia publishes draft AS/NZS 8700:2025 — *Critical Digital Infrastructure: Safety and Resilience Requirements* — within two weeks. Based on Sofia Ruiz's holonic architecture. Public comment period: 60 days. By week ten, the draft is finalised and published as official standard. Initially voluntary; mandatory Q1 2026 via regulation. Scope: systems affecting more than 100,000 users or critical life-safety functions. Requirements: manual overrides, offline operation, staged deployment, independent audits.

**Industry action.** AllConnex announces its *Safety First Initiative* — CEO Harrington's PR damage control plus genuine technical work, Amara Chen's team implementing holonic architecture. Pilot Q2, full rollout Q4 2025. Cost: $18 million — painful but necessary. By week six, an industry coalition forms: *Responsible Tech Australia*. Forty companies including competitors — Google, Amazon, Microsoft, local startups. Stated goal: self-regulation to avoid heavy-handed mandates. Skepticism justified: this is both lobbying strategy and partial reform. By week twelve, the first company achieves AS/NZS 8700 compliance — LocalFirst Home, the open-source startup. Marketing: *Australia's First Safety-Certified Smart Home.* Competitive advantage in customers choosing safety over features.

**Civil society action.** ADRC hits 100,000 members by week two. Formal incorporation as nonprofit. Crowdfunding $240k in the first month, foundation grants $180k. Five full-time staff — Miguel as Executive Director, reluctantly. Chapters in Melbourne, Brisbane, Perth, Adelaide, Darwin, regional hubs. By week eight, the first voluntary *Tech Failure Day* drills run. 12,000 households participate. Modest but meaningful. Media coverage: *Are Australians Ready for the Next Digital Disaster?* By week twelve, NSW becomes the first state to enshrine the Digital Consumer Bill of Rights in law. Key provisions: offline functionality, manual override, compensation rights. Enforcement: NSW Fair Trading. Real penalties: $10k per violation.

**Q2 metrics:** Essential Services Continuity Act passed. AS/NZS 8700 published. Technology Safety Commission operational. Industry compliance 8% — three companies certified, 35 in progress. Public awareness 23%.

#### Q3 2025 — Implementation Phase

**Government.** Technology Safety Commission audits commence. Forty companies selected on risk basis — high user count plus critical systems. Audit scope: architecture review, deployment procedures, incident response plans. Ninety days per audit. Public disclosure required: all audit findings published. By week 20, ACCC releases AllConnex interim findings — preliminary conclusion *unconscionable conduct, misleading representations, consumer protection violations.* Evidence: internal emails showing management overruled safety warnings; marketing promised reliability the architecture could not deliver. Proposed penalties: $85 million fine plus structural remedies. AllConnex response: settlement negotiations begin. By week 24, the first prosecution under new laws: SmartLocks Pty Ltd, not AllConnex (they are settling). Violation: sold *smart locks* with no manual override, no offline function. Customer case: elderly woman locked out, fell, injured. $180k fine, mandatory product recall, victim compensation. Message: the new regulations have teeth.

**Standards.** AS/NZS 8700 becomes mandatory via regulation gazetted in week 14. Compliance deadline 1 January 2026 — six months. Penalties: $50k per day for non-compliance, potential market ban. Ninety-day grace extension available for demonstrated good-faith effort. By week 22, David Okafor leads a Standards Australia delegation to Geneva. Meetings with ISO/IEC JTC 1 and IEEE. Outcome: ISO/IEC agrees to fast-track international version, projected 2026.

**Industry.** AllConnex completes its holonic architecture pilot in week 14: 50,000 users in Sydney metropolitan area. Results: 99.97% uptime, zero lock-outs, successful offline operation during three simulated failures. Amara Chen's team validated. Decision: full rollout approved, Q4 2025 completion. By week 18, Responsible Tech Australia publishes *Building Resilient Digital Infrastructure: Industry Best Practices.* Case studies, technical guides, cost-benefit analyses. Reception: genuine utility plus subtle lobbying. By week 24, major tech companies announce compliance updates. Google Australia: *Google Home devices now support offline routines.* Amazon: *Alexa devices maintain local voice control during outages.* Apple: *HomeKit enhanced with local processing, no cloud required for basic functions.* Competitive advantage: *Privacy plus Resilience equals Premium Product.*

**Civil society.** ADRC launches the *Know Your Tech Rights* app in week 16. Free mobile app: scans QR codes on smart devices, shows compliance status, explains user rights. Downloads: 140,000 in the first month. Funding: grants plus voluntary donations — sustainable model emerging. By week 20, community resilience networks reach 500 neighbourhoods. Tool libraries lending analog devices. Skill shares teaching lock-picking, manual thermostats. Mutual aid agreements. Participants report feeling 65% more confident handling tech failures. By week 24, the first *Resilient Community* certification: Brisbane's West End. Criteria: 70% of residents trained, community emergency plan, shared analog resources. Recognition from Queensland Government plus disaster preparedness funding. Goal: scale nationally.

**Q3 metrics:** Industry compliance 34%. Public awareness 47%. ACCC settlement in progress. Zero catastrophic failures since February.

#### Q4 2025 — Consolidation Phase

**Government.** Technology Safety Commission publishes first audit reports in week 28. Forty companies audited. Twelve fully compliant. Eighteen partially compliant. Ten non-compliant. Non-compliant given 60-day remediation notice or face market suspension. Media: *Tech Safety Report Card: Who's Protecting Users?* By week 32, AllConnex settlement is finalised. Terms: $85 million fine to consolidated revenue, portion allocated to digital resilience fund. Structural remedies: independent safety oversight board, annual third-party audits, five-year monitoring. CEO David Harrington resigns — negotiated departure, no admission of personal wrongdoing but takes responsibility. New CEO: external hire with engineering background plus safety culture experience. By week 36, Parliament committee reviews the Essential Services Continuity Act at six months. Findings: law working, but gaps remain — international platforms, emerging tech not covered. Recommendations: expand scope, increase enforcement budget, long-term monitoring.

**Standards.** AS/NZS 8700 compliance deadline approaching: 68% compliant, 24% requesting extensions, 8% non-compliant. Extensions granted case-by-case. Non-compliant products being removed from shelves — retailers avoiding liability. By week 36, the international standard ISO/IEC 27072:2025 is published — *Information Technology: Critical Digital Infrastructure Safety.* Based heavily on Australian AS/NZS 8700. David Okafor's coordination successful. EU announces adoption 2026. US considers, slower due to industry lobbying. Asia mixed.

**Industry.** AllConnex completes its nationwide rollout in week 28. All 2.4 million users migrated to holonic architecture. Migration mostly backend, minimal user disruption. Testing: simulated failures, offline mode works, manual overrides functional. Amara Chen promoted to Chief Technology Officer — she had refused until architecture was fixed; now it is, she accepts. By week 32, industry celebrates the *no major incidents* milestone. Eight months since AllConnex, zero catastrophic outages. Attribution: better architecture plus heightened awareness plus regulatory pressure. Skepticism: is this sustainable or temporary caution? Time will tell.

**Civil society.** ADRC annual report in week 32: membership 215,000 — from eight people in February. Impact: three state laws passed, 140 companies changed policies, 500-plus communities trained. Miguel Santos: *February felt like end of the world. Turned out to be the beginning of a movement.* By week 36, *Tech Failure Day* becomes annual tradition — December 17, 2025, marking ten months post-AllConnex. Participation: 180,000 households, 15x increase. Activities: practise manual systems, community drills, skill-sharing. Cultural shift: from *embarrassing necessity* to *civic responsibility.*

**End-of-year-one metrics:** Industry compliance 92%. Public awareness 71%. Zero catastrophic failures full year. ADRC membership 215,000. Legal framework established, enforced, refined.

#### 2026–2027 — Maturation and Global Expansion

January 2026: AS/NZS 8700 compliance universal — 99.2% of market. March: Technology Safety Commission expands mandate to AI systems and autonomous vehicles. June: EU adopts ISO/IEC 27072, creating trans-Pacific regulatory alignment. September: US Congress holds hearings on *Critical Digital Infrastructure Safety Act* modelled on Australian law. December: zero major failures, second consecutive year.

17 February 2027: two-year anniversary commemorated as *Digital Resilience Day,* like Safer Internet Day. April: David Okafor publishes *The Responsibility Revolution: How Australia Led the World in Technology Governance.* June: International Technology Standards Secretariat proposed at G20 — precursor to the Governance Compact summit five years later. October: Priya Sharma elected Prime Minister. Labor victory, platform includes digital rights. December: Miguel Santos receives UN Digital Rights Award.

Cumulative impact 2025–2027: $12 billion invested in resilience infrastructure. $180 billion in estimated prevented losses. 2.1 million Australians trained in digital resilience. 3,200 communities certified resilient. Eight countries adopt the Australian model. Forty-plus studying for adaptation. Cultural shift from *tech will save us* to *tech is a tool — we save ourselves.*

### Alternative Futures

GC-BAT's framework included scenario planning — not predictions, possibilities. How different choices lead to different outcomes.

#### Scenario A — The Resilient Commonwealth (probability ~35%)

Governments, industry, and civil society fully commit to reform. Australia becomes global leader in technology governance.

2025–2027: rapid implementation as described above.

2028–2030: expansion and deepening. All critical infrastructure — power, water, transport, health, finance — required to meet resilience standards. Australia exports governance model through bilateral agreements, capacity building, technical assistance. Domestic innovation flourishes. Local companies like LocalFirst Home compete globally on *safety-certified* branding. Cultural transformation: *Built in Australia* becomes synonymous with *built to last.*

By 2032: Australia co-hosts International Governance Compact in Sydney. Priya Sharma as PM and Marcus Bennett as ITSS Director lead negotiations. Forty nations sign framework based on Australian model. AllConnex, rebranded and reformed, is case study in *how companies transform after crisis.* Miguel Santos, Amara Chen, David Okafor, others — international advisors, keynote speakers, living proof that advocacy works.

Indicators: zero catastrophic digital failures 2025–2032. 95% public confidence in critical digital systems — highest globally. Australian tech exports increase 340% — resilience as competitive advantage. Digital inequality gap reduced 68% — equity protections working.

What made this possible: political will sustained beyond election cycles; industry embraced responsibility — profit and purpose; civil society maintained pressure with accountability mechanisms; international cooperation prevented regulatory arbitrage; public trust restored through transparency plus results.

Optimistic but achievable. Requires sustained commitment across stakeholders. History shows reform momentum often fades after initial crisis response. But Australia has demonstrated capacity for long-term policy vision — gun reform post-Port Arthur, pandemic response. If framed as national identity issue — *Australia: where technology serves people* — cultural staying power increases.

#### Scenario B — The Fragmented Patchwork (probability ~45%)

Reforms happen but inconsistently. Some progress, but gaps remain. Improvement without transformation.

2025–2026: initial strong action as described.

2027–2029: reform momentum slows. Industry lobbying weakens enforcement. Budget cuts reduce Technology Safety Commission capacity. International coordination stalls — US-China tensions prevent global standards. Civil society fatigue — advocacy groups struggle with sustained funding. New crises distract: climate disasters, economic recession, geopolitical tensions.

2030: Australian systems more resilient than 2025, but not as safe as they could be. Compliance culture weakens — companies do *minimum viable safety.* Regional variation — NSW and Victoria strong enforcement; other states laxer. No catastrophic failures, but regular minor incidents — smaller-scale AllConnex-style outages. Public acceptance: *Better than it was, not as good as it should be.*

2032 (capstone): no single Governance Compact. Multiple regional agreements instead. Australia participates but is not leader — EU takes charge; US and China do their own things. Domestic reforms preserved but not expanded. Original advocates — Miguel, Amara — still involved but frustrated by incomplete progress.

Indicators: three to five major digital disruptions 2025–2032, smaller than AllConnex but significant. 62% public confidence — moderate trust. Australian tech exports increase 80% — some advantage, not dominant. Digital inequality gap reduced 30% — progress, but insufficient.

Most likely scenario based on historical patterns. Reforms typically follow crisis but rarely achieve full vision. Political cycles, industry resistance, and competing priorities dilute initial momentum. This scenario delivers improvement — measurably better than 2025 baseline — but falls short of transformation. Public gets *good enough* rather than *excellent.* Risk: *good enough* creates complacency, vulnerability to future shocks that exceed improved but incomplete resilience.

#### Scenario C — The Recurring Crisis (probability ~20%)

Reforms fail to take hold. Industry resistance, political dysfunction, or public apathy prevent meaningful change. AllConnex becomes template, not warning.

2025–2026: initial reform efforts meet fierce resistance. Industry coalition launches massive lobbying campaign: *Regulation kills innovation.* Political polarisation: reform becomes partisan issue. Essential Services Continuity Act passes but weakened. No enforcement teeth. Technology Safety Commission underfunded, understaffed, captured by industry. Standards Australia pressured to soften AS/NZS 8700.

2027: momentum collapses. ACCC settlement with AllConnex reduced on appeal — $85M to $12M. New CEO quietly reverses safety investments. Civil society groups struggle with funding. Media cycle moves on. New technologies — AI agents, BCI, quantum — deployed without safety frameworks.

2028–2030: second crisis. Major failure occurs. Not AllConnex but similar architecture flaw in different platform. National payment system crashes — Story #5 scale. Eight-hour outage. $6 billion economic impact. Or: healthcare AI system failure causes 40-plus deaths — Story #6 but worse. Or: autonomous vehicle fleet coordination failure, mass casualties — Story #7 but catastrophic. Public outrage returns. *Why didn't we learn from AllConnex?* Answer: we tried, but reforms were blocked.

2032: multiple crises — 2025, 2028, 2031 — create pattern of failure. Australia becomes cautionary tale: *They had the chance to lead, but chose short-term profit over long-term safety.* No Governance Compact. International community fragments. Technology backlash. Public loses trust. Adoption slows. Innovation suffers. Insurance premiums skyrocket. Productivity losses. Capital flight. Digital divide widens. Vulnerable populations suffer repeatedly. Political instability — governments fall over tech failures.

Indicators: 12–18 major digital disruptions 2025–2032 — recurring failures, escalating severity. 28% public confidence — deep distrust. Australian tech exports decrease 15% — damaged reputation. Digital inequality gap increases 25% — regressive outcomes.

Pessimistic but plausible. Historical precedent: financial crisis 2008 — strong initial reform push, gradual rollback over decade, vulnerabilities rebuilt. Difference: tech failures affect daily life more directly than financial abstractions. Public will not tolerate recurring lock-outs. But 20% probability remains — if reform fails early, cynicism sets in, each subsequent crisis normalised rather than galvanising. Most dangerous outcome: not dramatic collapse but slow degradation of safety, resilience, trust.

### Metrics of Success

Quantitative and qualitative. Testable, transparent, trackable.

**Technical.** *Catastrophic failure rate* — baseline 2024: 1.8 per year affecting >100k users; target 2027: <0.5 per year. *System resilience score* — % of critical systems meeting AS/NZS 8700: baseline 3%; target 90% by 2026. *Recovery time objective achievement* — % of outages resolved within 4-hour RTO: baseline 52%; target 95% by 2027. *Manual override availability* — % of smart devices with functional manual operation: baseline 12%; target 100% critical, 80% all systems by 2026.

**Social.** *Public trust index* — survey: *I trust that critical digital systems will work when I need them.* Baseline February 2025 post-AllConnex: 31%; target 2027: 75%. *Digital resilience literacy* — % of population able to operate household systems manually. Baseline 18%; target 70% by 2027. *Community resilience networks* — number of certified resilient communities. Baseline 0; target 5,000 by 2027.

**Equity.** *Restoration equity index* — ratio of restoration times (wealthy areas / vulnerable areas); target 1.0. Baseline 2.7; target 1.2 by 2027. *Accessibility compliance* — % of smart systems maintaining disability access during outages. Baseline 8%; target 95% by 2026. *Multilingual support availability* — % of critical systems offering emergency support in top 10 community languages. Baseline 15%; target 90% by 2026.

**Economic.** *Cost of failure prevented* — estimated losses prevented by improved resilience. Baseline 2024: $0; target 2027: $15B-plus cumulative. *Resilience investment ROI* — prevented losses / investment cost. Investment 2025–2027: $12B; target ratio >5:1.

**Qualitative.** *Corporate safety culture* — assessment of whether companies prioritise safety alongside profit. Baseline 2024: *move fast, break things.* Target 2027: *build safe, stay accountable* as industry norm. *Political will sustainability* — whether regulatory commitment survives election cycles, budget pressures. Baseline: no political will (no crisis, no attention). Target: sustained bipartisan commitment. *International influence* — number of countries adopting Australian-model frameworks. Baseline 0; target 15 by 2027.

GC-BAT published a public dashboard, updated quarterly. End of 2025 dashboard: nine indicators green (on track or ahead) — catastrophic failure rate, system resilience, public trust, digital literacy, cost prevented, multilingual support, international influence, community networks, RTO achievement. Four yellow (progress but behind target) — manual override availability, restoration equity, accessibility compliance, resilience investment ROI (early stage). Two red (concerning gaps) — corporate safety culture (mixed/uncertain), political will sustainability (untested).

Interpretation: strong first-year progress, but critical areas need attention. Equity gaps persistent. Cultural and political metrics harder to shift than technical metrics.

### Final Reflection

Marcus Bennett's closing words in the GC-BAT framework, published Monday morning:

> *The AllConnex failure of February 17, 2025 revealed a truth we'd been avoiding: our technological sophistication has outpaced our governance wisdom. We built systems that work brilliantly — until they don't. We optimised for convenience and profit — but not for resilience and equity.*
>
> *This framework provides a path forward. Not a guarantee, but a possibility. The choice is ours — collectively, as governments, companies, communities, and individuals.*
>
> *We can choose Scenario A: transform crisis into catalyst, build resilient systems, lead globally. This requires sustained commitment, resources, and courage to prioritise long-term safety over short-term profit.*
>
> *We can drift toward Scenario B: make improvements but not transformation. Deliver good enough but not excellent. This is easier — requires less political capital, less corporate sacrifice, less public engagement. But it leaves vulnerabilities.*
>
> *Or we can fail into Scenario C: let reform momentum collapse, normalise recurring crises, repeat our mistakes. This happens not through malice but through inattention — when we move on to the next news cycle, the next priority, the next distraction.*
>
> *History suggests Scenario B is most likely. But history is not destiny. We write our future through choices made today.*
>
> *To governments: you have the power to regulate. Use it wisely, consistently, courageously.*
>
> *To companies: you have the responsibility to serve society, not just shareholders. Profit and safety are not opposites — they're interdependent.*
>
> *To civil society: you have the voice to demand better. Organise, advocate, persist.*
>
> *To citizens: you have the right to resilient systems. Expect them, demand them, support those building them.*
>
> *And to all of us: remember February 17, 2025. Remember what it felt like when technology failed us. Remember that we're not helpless — we're temporarily disconnected from our power. Reconnect. Act.*
>
> *The subscription disconnect was one crisis. The governance compact — connecting safety, equity, and innovation — is our response.*
>
> *Let's build systems worthy of the people who depend on them.*
>
> *Let's build a future where technology serves humanity, not the other way around.*
