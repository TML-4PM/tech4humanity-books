## Part 2 — Stakeholder Universe

### Government & Regulators — Parliament House, Canberra, Friday 2:00 PM

Priya Sharma sat in the back of the House of Representatives chamber, watching Question Time descend into chaos.

She was a backbencher — technically Shadow Parliamentary Secretary for Digital Economy, which meant she had a title but limited power. Her role was to ask questions the Opposition wanted asked, highlight government failures, build her reputation.

Today she did not need to ask. The questions were asking themselves.

"Mr. Speaker, will the Prime Minister explain to the 2.4 million Australians who lost access to essential services this morning why his government has failed to regulate subscription-based infrastructure?" Labor frontbencher, voice sharp.

The Prime Minister stood. "Mr. Speaker, this is a private-sector failure, not a government policy failure. AllConnex is a commercial entity. The market—"

Interruption from the crossbench: "The market locked grandmothers in their apartments! The market killed livestock! The market crashed hospitals!"

Speaker: "Order! The member will wait to be recognised!"

But the chamber was already past order. MPs from all sides were shouting — not at each other, but at the government, demanding answers, accountability, action.

Priya watched. Listened. Calculated.

This was opportunity. Not cynical political opportunity, though she was ambitious enough to recognise that too. Real opportunity. The kind that happens when a system fails so spectacularly that the public demands change and politicians have no choice but to deliver.

She pulled out her tablet, opened her notes app, started drafting:

> **Framework: Subscription Infrastructure as Critical Utility**
>
> If 2.4M households depend on a service for basic life functions (security, climate, communication), that service is *de facto* infrastructure. Infrastructure requires regulation.
>
> **Proposal: "Essential Services Continuity Act"**
> — Subscription services affecting >100k households must meet utility-grade reliability standards
> — Mandatory fail-safe mechanisms (manual overrides, offline operation modes)
> — Independent oversight body (ACCC expanded mandate?)
> — Penalties for catastrophic failures: not just fines — criminal liability for executives

She kept typing. Two thousand words in twenty minutes. Fifteen specific policy recommendations. Three legislative pathways. Timeline for implementation.

When Question Time ended — no answers given, just political theatre — Priya walked to the Opposition Leader's office.

Knocked. Entered without waiting for invitation. She had been Chief of Staff here two years ago, before getting elected herself.

"Priya." The Leader looked exhausted. "I assume you have thoughts."

"I have a bill," Priya said, placing her tablet on the desk. "Ready for introduction Monday. Essential Services Continuity Act. Bipartisan language — focuses on safety, not politics. Crossbench will support it. Government will resist but eventually cave because the optics are impossible."

The Leader skimmed it. Looked up. "You wrote this in ninety minutes?"

"I've been thinking about this problem for two years. Today just made it politically viable."

"If I sponsor this, you'll be visible. Media will call you tech guru, future minister. You comfortable with that?"

Priya did not hesitate. "I'm comfortable with preventing the next 2.4 million people from being locked out of their lives because a server crashed."

The Leader smiled. "Monday introduction. You'll be secondary sponsor. Start briefing crossbench tonight."

By 5:00 PM Priya had commitments from four crossbench MPs. By 8:00 PM she had drafted media talking points. By 11:00 PM she had written an op-ed for tomorrow's *Sydney Morning Herald:* *Why Your Smart Home Is Critical Infrastructure — And Must Be Regulated As Such.*

She filed it. Went home. Slept three hours. Woke to her phone ringing.

Marcus Bennett. GC-BAT.

She had heard of them — independent think tank, technology governance, respected by both industry and civil society. Rare combination.

"Ms. Sharma," Marcus's voice was calm, measured. "I'm watching your work on the Continuity Act. It's good. Want to make it better?"

"I'm listening."

"We've been mapping subscription infrastructure vulnerabilities for eighteen months. I can send you technical specifications for fail-safe requirements — not vague *reliability standards,* but specific engineering mandates. Testable. Auditable. That's what your bill needs to survive industry pushback."

Priya was silent for three seconds. Thinking. Calculating trust.

"Send them. If they're good, I'll incorporate them. If you're trying to weaken the bill, I'll know, and you'll regret it."

Marcus laughed. "Ms. Sharma, we want your bill stronger, not weaker. Industry is going to fight. You need technical credibility. We'll provide it."

He sent the specs. Priya read them. They were good. Better than good. Exactly what her bill needed.

She incorporated them. Monday's introduction would include GC-BAT's engineering standards.

And Priya Sharma, backbencher from Western Sydney, would become the politician who turned a technological disaster into legislative reform.

### Industry — AllConnex Headquarters, Sydney CBD, Friday 4:00 PM

The executive floor conference room — *Boardroom A* in corporate speak — was designed for power. Floor-to-ceiling windows overlooking Circular Quay. Herman Miller chairs. A table that could seat twenty-four. Screens embedded in the walls.

Right now, it felt like a tribunal.

Dr. Amara Chen sat at the end of the table, flanked by Sarah Mitchell and James Park. Facing them: CEO David Harrington, CFO, COO, General Counsel, and Head of Communications.

David Harrington — mid-50s, Stanford MBA, twenty-year tech veteran, three IPOs, reputation as *pragmatic visionary* — looked like he had not slept. Because he had not. He had spent the last eight hours on calls with investors, regulators, media, lawyers.

"Dr. Chen," he started, voice controlled but strained, "walk me through what happened. No technical jargon — I need to explain this to the board."

Amara opened her laptop. Pulled up the failure timeline she had compiled. Spoke clearly, precisely:

"March 2024. I proposed distributed subscription validation with local caching and manual overrides. Cost: $2.3 million. Strategic decision was made to maintain centralised architecture for platform lock-in. That decision created the single point of failure.

"Yesterday, executive approval overrode standard code review process to push a fraud detection update. Update contained logic error. At 3:14 AM today, deployment initiated. At 6:30 AM, error activated — inverted conditional check — all active subscriptions flagged as inactive.

"By 6:45 AM, 2.4 million users locked out. Recovery delayed because database locked mid-deployment. Database team recalled, manual intervention, rollback at 9:47 AM. Full service restoration by 1:00 PM."

She closed the laptop. "That's what happened. Cause: architectural fragility plus rushed deployment. Prevention: adopt the architecture I proposed eight months ago."

David looked at her. Then at General Counsel, who was typing frantically on her laptop.

"Dr. Chen," General Counsel said carefully, "do you have documentation of your March proposal?"

"Yes. Email chain. Architecture specs. Risk assessment. Meeting minutes where the proposal was rejected with explicit reasoning: *Platform lock-in is strategic priority.*" Amara's voice was calm. Too calm. "I also have documentation of my warnings that this exact failure mode was possible."

General Counsel's face went pale.

David Harrington closed his eyes. "Are you planning to share that documentation publicly?"

"That depends," Amara said, "on what AllConnex does next."

Silence.

"Specifically: are we implementing distributed architecture? Are we requiring manual overrides on all critical systems? Are we establishing independent safety review boards? Are we committing to industry-wide standards?"

David looked at the CFO. The CFO shook his head slightly — translation: *expensive.*

"Dr. Chen, I appreciate your thoroughness. But we need to consider—"

"No," Amara interrupted. "You need to decide whether you're going to fix the problem or manage the optics. Because if it's optics, I'm out. And my documentation goes with me."

The room froze.

Sarah Mitchell, sitting beside Amara, looked shocked — she had never seen her boss confront executives like this. James Park looked calm. He had been expecting this.

David Harrington stood. Walked to the window. Looked out at the harbour.

"If we implement everything you're proposing," he said, not turning around, "costs increase forty percent. Competitive advantage decreases. Stock takes a hit."

"If we don't," Amara said, "this happens again. Except next time, someone dies. And when they investigate, they'll find I warned you. They'll find you chose profits over safety. How's your stock then?"

David turned around. Looked at Amara. Really looked — not at an employee, but at a problem he could not optimise away.

"Draft your technical requirements. You have one week. Make them implementable — I need timelines, costs, migration paths. If the board approves, we do it."

"And if they don't?"

"Then I guess you'll be sharing that documentation."

Amara stood. "One week."

She left. Sarah and James followed.

In the elevator, Sarah whispered: "Amara, did you just threaten to blow up the company?"

"No. I threatened to tell the truth. There's a difference."

Across the broader tech sector, the failure cascaded beyond AllConnex.

By evening, every major tech company in Australia was in crisis mode — not because their systems had failed (most had not), but because the public suddenly questioned whether they could.

Google Australia: emergency all-hands. Question: if AllConnex could fail, what about Gmail? Google Home? Android device management? Answer: distributed systems with redundancy. But how to prove that to regulators and users?

AWS Sydney region: customer calls exploding. Multi-region architecture existed, but customers needed to configure it correctly. Most did not.

Microsoft Azure Australia: similar panic. Teams mobilising to document resilience architecture, preparing white papers, planning customer education campaigns.

Local startups: terror. If regulators imposed AllConnex-style requirements on small companies, compliance costs could kill businesses. Industry associations drafting position papers: *Don't punish innovation because of one company's failure.*

By Saturday morning, TechCouncil Australia had scheduled an emergency board meeting. By Sunday, they had drafted a public statement:

> *We support appropriate safety standards for consumer technology. However, we caution against reactionary regulation that could stifle innovation and harm Australia's technology sector...*

Translation: *Please don't make us spend money on safety.*

But the public was not listening. The outrage was too raw. And politicians were already drafting bills.

### Standards Bodies — Standards Australia Headquarters, Sydney, Monday 10:00 AM

The emergency working group meeting had twenty-three attendees on Zoom, eight in person.

David Okafor attended in person. He had flown from Canberra, where he had been testifying at a Senate committee on an unrelated AI ethics matter, specifically for this.

The chair — Dr. Patricia Wong, Head of ICT Standards — opened:

"Colleagues, I've called this session to address the AllConnex incident. Three days ago, 2.4 million Australians lost access to critical infrastructure due to a software failure. The question before us: Do we have adequate standards? If not, what do we develop?"

David raised his hand. "Patricia, with respect — we have standards. AS/NZS ISO/IEC 27001 for information security. ISO 22301 for business continuity. The problem isn't absence of standards. It's absence of enforcement."

Murmurs of agreement.

Another participant, industry representative: "David, these standards are voluntary. Companies adopt them for competitive advantage, not legal obligation. You're proposing mandatory compliance?"

"I'm proposing," David said carefully, "that when a service becomes critical infrastructure — and 2.4 million dependencies makes it critical — voluntary standards are insufficient. We need regulatory mandate."

Patricia jumped in: "David's raising the key tension. Standards bodies develop best practices. Governments enforce them. Our question: What best practices do we recommend?"

The group spent three hours debating. David took notes:

> **Proposed Standards (Emergency Working Group):**
>
> 1. **Fail-Safe Architecture Standard** — All systems affecting >100k users must have offline operation mode; manual overrides for life-safety systems; redundant authentication.
>
> 2. **Deployment Safety Standard** — Critical updates require staged rollout (5% → 25% → 100%); automated rollback triggers for >1% error rate; mandatory testing environment that mirrors production.
>
> 3. **Transparency & Auditability** — Public disclosure of architecture resilience; independent third-party audits annually; incident reporting within 2 hours of detection.
>
> 4. **Recovery Time Objective** — Critical systems: 4-hour maximum outage. Non-critical: 24-hour maximum. Escalating penalties for breach.

David listened. Added notes. Then raised a point:

"These are good. But we're missing the process standard. AllConnex's failure wasn't just technical — it was organisational. Executive override of safety review. That's governance failure."

Patricia nodded. "You're right. We need a governance standard."

"And we need international coordination. Australia can't set standards that conflict with IEEE, ISO, EU frameworks. We'd fragment the global tech ecosystem."

Patricia assigned David to chair a sub-working group: International Coordination on Technology Resilience Standards. Mission: coordinate with IEEE, ISO/IEC JTC 1, ETSI.

David accepted. This was his specialty — not just technical standards, but diplomatic standards. Making sure the global system did not fracture into incompatible regional requirements.

After the meeting, Patricia pulled David aside:

"I'm recommending you to the ACCC as technical advisor. They're investigating AllConnex for potential consumer protection violations. Your testimony would be valuable."

"I'll do it. On one condition: my testimony goes public. Full transparency. No NDAs protecting corporate interests over public safety."

"ACCC will agree to that. You'd be expert witness, not consultant."

"Then I'm in."

David left the building, walked to a nearby café, ordered coffee, opened his laptop, and started writing:

> **Subscription-Based Critical Infrastructure: A Framework for Regulatory Oversight and Technical Standards**
>
> *Abstract: The AllConnex incident of February 2025 exposed fundamental vulnerabilities in the subscription economy model when applied to critical infrastructure. This paper proposes an integrated framework combining technical standards (fail-safe architecture, deployment safety, RTO requirements) with governance standards (executive accountability, safety review processes, incident transparency). We argue that voluntary adoption is insufficient for systems affecting >100k users and recommend regulatory mandate backed by international standards coordination...*

He would publish this in *Journal of Cyber Policy* within two weeks. It would become foundational text for regulatory frameworks globally.

But right now, he just wrote. Because someone had to document the solution before the next crisis.

### Civil Society — CHOICE Office, Sydney, Monday 9:00 AM

Miguel Santos walked into the cramped conference room, and forty-seven people looked up at him.

He had not expected forty-seven. The invitation said *8–10 interested community members.* Apparently, rage scaled faster than predictions.

"Uh, hi," Miguel said, suddenly very aware that he was wearing the same shirt as Friday. "I'm Miguel. I — organised this?"

A woman in her sixties raised her hand. "I'm Carol. Locked in my apartment Friday morning. My son had to break down my door. I'm furious. What do we do?"

And that was it. The dam broke.

For two hours, people shared stories. James, a disability advocate: smart wheelchair lost connectivity, stranded him mid-transit. Sarah, single mother: work-from-home job, could not access her computer because the smart desk (yes, those existed) would not unlock. Ahmed, small business owner: point-of-sale system offline, could not process transactions, lost a full day's revenue. Priya, healthcare worker: hospital shift, could not reach patients because key card access was down. Marcus, student: could not submit university assignment because cloud storage failed.

Every story had the same theme: *I trusted this technology. The technology broke. I had no backup. I was helpless.*

Miguel listened. Took notes. And realised this was not just about AllConnex. This was about a decade of *move fast and break things* finally breaking people.

After the meeting, eight core members stayed. They founded a working group: **Australian Digital Rights Coalition (ADRC)**.

Miguel became co-chair, reluctantly — he had never led anything, but everyone insisted.

Their first action: draft a Digital Consumer Bill of Rights.

Miguel wrote it in three days, consulting with legal advisors (pro bono from community legal centres), tech experts (including a helpful email exchange with Dr. Amara Chen, who had somehow gotten his contact), and dozens of affected consumers.

> **Digital Consumer Bill of Rights (Draft 1.0)**
>
> *Preamble: When digital services become essential to daily life, consumers have rights beyond traditional market protections. This bill establishes minimum standards for technology that affects health, safety, security, and essential communication.*
>
> **Article I — Right to Offline Functionality.** Consumers have the right to basic operation of purchased devices without constant internet connectivity. Essential features (locks, climate control, safety systems) must function offline.
>
> **Article II — Right to Manual Override.** Consumers have the right to manual control mechanisms for all automated systems. No device may exclusively rely on digital control where failure creates safety risk.
>
> **Article III — Right to Data Portability.** Consumers have the right to export their data in standard formats and migrate to alternative services without penalty or loss of functionality.
>
> **Article IV — Right to Transparent Failure Modes.** Companies must disclose how systems behave during outages, what functionality is lost, and what backup options exist. This information must be provided at point of sale.
>
> **Article V — Right to Timely Notice.** Consumers have the right to immediate notification of service disruptions affecting safety, security, or essential functions.
>
> **Article VI — Right to Compensation.** Consumers have the right to compensation for service failures that cause demonstrable harm. Companies cannot waive liability for catastrophic failures through terms of service.
>
> **Article VII — Right to Exit.** Consumers have the right to discontinue service and remove company access to their devices, data, and property. No company may *brick* consumer-owned devices due to subscription cancellation.
>
> **Article VIII — Right to Privacy in Crisis.** During system failures, companies may not use crisis circumstances to collect additional data or modify terms of service.
>
> **Article IX — Right to Independent Oversight.** Consumers have the right to report safety concerns to independent regulators without retaliation.
>
> **Article X — Right to Collective Action.** Consumers have the right to organise, share information about service failures, and pursue collective legal action. Companies cannot require individual arbitration for class-action safety claims.

Miguel published it online. Within 24 hours: 12,000 signatures. Within 48 hours: 40,000. Within a week: 200,000 signatures, coverage in every major Australian media outlet, and an invitation to testify before Parliament.

The morning he testified — Wednesday, eight days post-crisis — he wore the same shirt. Deliberately. Because he wanted MPs to remember: this started with an ordinary person in an apartment, locked out, scared, organising neighbours.

His testimony was five minutes. He did not read the Bill of Rights. He told stories. Carol locked in her apartment. James stranded in his wheelchair. The mother whose toddler cried for her AI friend. Mr. Chen from Unit 604, whose broken door still had not been fixed because the building manager said it was *tenant damage.*

"These are your constituents," Miguel said, looking at each MP. "They trusted technology that failed them. They bought devices marketed as *smart* that became stupid — or worse, dangerous — when a server crashed. They deserve better. We deserve better.

"The Digital Consumer Bill of Rights isn't radical. It's basic safety. Offline functionality. Manual overrides. Transparent failure modes. These are seatbelts for the digital age. You mandated seatbelts in cars because crashes kill people. Digital crashes are killing trust, livelihoods, and — if we're not careful — they'll kill people too."

He sat down. The room was silent.

Then Priya Sharma asked: "Mr. Santos, if Parliament adopted your Bill of Rights, would it prevent another AllConnex?"

Miguel thought. "No. Nothing prevents all failures. But it would give people tools to survive failures. Backup options. Manual controls. Actual recourse. Right now, when systems fail, consumers are helpless. Your Bill of Rights means they're not."

Priya nodded. "Thank you. I'll be incorporating several of your articles into the Essential Services Continuity Act."

That evening, sitting in a pub with the eight core ADRC members, someone asked: "Miguel, what's next?"

He looked at his phone. 847 unread emails. Interview requests. Speaking invitations. Partnership proposals.

"Next we make sure this isn't just a moment. We build a movement. Because AllConnex won't be the last failure. And next time, we need to be ready."

They clinked glasses. The Australian Digital Rights Coalition was born.

### Public — Across Australia, Friday Through Sunday

The public response was not monolithic. It was fractal — millions of individual reactions that, in aggregate, revealed something deeper about technology's role in modern life.

Twitter/X was on fire. Literally — the platform's servers struggled under load as #SubDisconnect, #AllConnexFail, and #SmartHomeFail trended globally.

> **@TechFurious:** I PAID $4,200 FOR A "SMART HOME" THAT JUST LOCKED ME OUT OF MY OWN HOUSE. EXPLAIN HOW THIS IS LEGAL.
>
> **@JusticeSeeker88:** AllConnex execs better be going to jail. This isn't an oopsie. This is negligence. People could have died.
>
> **@ConsumerRage2025:** Remember when we just had KEYS? And LIGHT SWITCHES? And they WORKED? Gonna rip out every "smart" device and go full analog. Done with this dystopian bullshit.

Memes spread: a person using a manual screwdriver to open a door, captioned *The most advanced technology of 2025.* An old Nokia phone: *Still works. Still charges. Still makes calls. No subscriptions. Be like Nokia.* A smart speaker displaying an error message: *Sorry, I can't help with that. (And also everything else.)*

Reddit threads dissected the failure technically. The top post on r/technology — "AllConnex Post-Mortem: How A Single Conditional Check Took Down 2.4M Homes" — collected 4,200 comments. Top reply, 8,400 upvotes: *This is why centralised auth is dangerous. Every security researcher warned about this. Every exec ignored them. Every consumer paid the price.* On r/homeautomation a tutorial — *Time to talk about local control and Home Assistant* — became gospel, spawning 40+ migration testimonials.

Parenting forums exploded. *My daughter's medical monitor went offline. She has a heart condition. I didn't know how to check her vitals without the app. I'm a nurse. I should know this. But I've relied on the device for so long I forgot the manual signs. I'm terrified of my own incompetence.*

Top reply, 427 upvotes: *You're not incompetent. You're a victim of technology that promised to make us safer but actually made us dependent. Not your fault. It's by design.*

Elderly community groups echoed: *I'm 76. My children set up my smart home because they worry about me living alone. Friday, I couldn't unlock my door. Couldn't adjust heating. Couldn't call for help because my phone was inside and the door lock was outside. I sat on my porch for four hours until my neighbour came home. I've never felt so helpless.*

Local community responses mirrored Miguel's building. In Melbourne's Brunswick: a neighbourhood group established *analog backup buddies* — pairs of neighbours who would check on each other during tech failures. In Brisbane's West End: a community centre started *Tech Resilience Workshops* — teaching manual overrides, offline alternatives, basic troubleshooting. In Perth's Fremantle: a local library created an *Emergency Tech Failure Kit* lending program — physical keys, battery radios, printed maps, manual tools.

Facebook groups formed: *AllConnex Survivors Support Group* (18,000 members in three days). *Smart Home Skeptics Australia* (8,000). *DIY Home Automation (No Cloud Needed)* (12,000).

Not everyone was outraged. Some were just tired. *Look, it sucked. But what are you gonna do? Go back to the stone age? Technology fails sometimes. Cars break down. Pipes burst. This is just modern infrastructure. We adapt.* These voices were quieter — not fewer, but quieter. Outrage is louder than resignation, and trauma more shareable than inconvenience.

Within 72 hours, startups launched. *FailSafe Home:* "Complete smart-home systems with guaranteed offline functionality." Seed funding: $2M in 48 hours. *BackupKey:* physical key + digital lock integration. *LocalFirst Home:* open-source smart-home platform, no cloud dependency. GitHub stars: 12,000 in four days.

Think pieces appeared in every publication. *The Guardian:* "The AllConnex Crisis and the Myth of Technological Progress." *The Age:* "When Smart Homes Make Us Stupid: The Case for Analog Backup." *ABC News Analysis:* "Subscription Economy or Subscription Dependency?"

By Sunday evening, surveys revealed public sentiment.

> **YouGov Australia Poll (n=2,847):**
>
> - Trust in smart-home technology: down 47%
> - Willingness to pay premium for offline-capable devices: 73%
> - Support for government regulation of subscription services: 81%
> - Belief that "technology companies prioritise profits over safety": 89%
> - Would consider removing smart-home devices: 34%
> - Actually removed devices since Friday: 12%

The gap between *would consider* (34%) and *actually did* (12%) revealed the core tension: people were angry but also trapped. Removing smart-home systems meant losing convenience, voiding warranties, sometimes losing property value. Dependency was not just psychological — it was financial, legal, social.

And then there were the quiet stories. A grandfather in Cairns who missed his grandson's birthday video call because his only device was a smart display that went offline. A student in Adelaide who failed an exam because her smart alarm did not wake her. A couple in Hobart who had their first major fight because the husband blamed the wife for choosing AllConnex. A small business owner in Darwin — not Theo, someone else — who lost $8,000 in inventory because his smart freezer went offline and did not alert him until spoilage was complete. A therapy patient whose AI mental-health companion went offline mid-session, who spiralled because the AI had become, genuinely, their primary emotional support.

These were not headlines. They were human costs.

And collectively, they added up to something unmeasurable but real: erosion of trust. Not just in AllConnex. In the promise of technology itself.

For a decade, the narrative had been: *Technology makes life better.*

Friday added an asterisk: *When it works.*

And people started asking: *What happens when it doesn't?*
