## Part 2 — Stakeholder Universe

### Government & Regulators — Senate Committee Room 2S1, Parliament House, Canberra, 2:00 PM

Senator Margaret Hayes, Liberal, Tasmania, chaired the Senate Standing Committees on Foreign Affairs, Defence and Trade. She had been in the chair seven months. She had spent forty-one years before that as a barrister specialising in administrative law, which meant she understood, in a way few senators did, the difference between a system that was unlawful and a system that was unaccountable. Most of her career had been spent on the latter. *Cloud Custody*, the title the secretariat had already started using for the inquiry, smelled to her like the latter as well.

She opened the session at 2:00 PM exactly, which her staff had told her was an exotic choice for a Senate committee, and called the first witness: Professor David Okafor, Director, ANU Centre for Responsible Technology.

David sat down at the witness table. He had not slept. He had spent the morning with his team and then ninety minutes with Aisha, during which she had made him better tea than he had made her, and they had agreed on a division of labour: he would give the Senate the diagnosis, she would give the Senate the diplomatic boundary conditions. The committee would have to provide the politics itself.

He read his opening statement.

"Chair, in 2023 I published a working paper titled *Cloud Sovereignty as Foreign Policy Risk.* I am not here today to take a victory lap. I am here because the failure mode I described two years ago has arrived, and we have approximately seventy-two hours before the public conversation about it ossifies in directions that will not serve the long-term interests of this country.

"The technical situation is straightforward. The United States has, by lawful executive action, directed its hyperscale cloud providers to implement compliance controls that have the side effect of degrading non-US workloads sharing the affected infrastructure. The Commonwealth of Australia is one of those non-US customers. We are experiencing degradation across approximately forty-seven Commonwealth services that I have identified by 1:00 PM today. The actual number is almost certainly higher.

"The contractual situation is also straightforward. Australia has no contractual remedy. The hyperscalers' standard terms permit them to manage capacity in response to lawful regulatory requirements in any jurisdiction in which they operate. Australia agreed to these terms when each Commonwealth agency procured cloud services. There is no breach.

"The constitutional situation is the question this committee will need to address. A sovereign nation has, as a matter of basic capability, the ability to operate the systems on which its government depends. Australia does not, at this moment, possess that ability. We possess instead the contractual right to access systems operated by entities subject to foreign sovereign authority. That is not sovereignty. That is custody."

He paused.

"I propose three immediate actions and one structural reform. The immediate actions: First, the Commonwealth must establish, within thirty days, a complete inventory of every Commonwealth workload running on foreign hyperscaler infrastructure, classified by criticality. We do not currently have this inventory. We are flying blind. Second, the Commonwealth must designate, by regulation, a category of *Sovereign-Critical Workload* — services whose degradation constitutes a sovereignty risk — and establish a mandatory migration timeline for those workloads to architectures that are either domestically operated or jurisdictionally diversified. Third, the Commonwealth must, by emergency regulation, require that any new procurement of cloud services for Commonwealth use include both a sovereignty assessment and a contractual right to capacity prioritisation that survives the provider's compliance with foreign regulatory direction. I am aware this third item will be resisted by the providers. The resistance will be informative.

"The structural reform: Australia must establish a Sovereign Cloud Capability Programme — a long-term investment, on the order of two to three billion dollars over five years, to build domestic capability in critical cloud, compute, and AI infrastructure. Not to displace the hyperscalers. To provide the option not to depend on them. Sovereignty is, in this domain as in all others, the ability to choose."

He stopped. He drank water from the glass the secretariat had provided. The committee room was very quiet.

Senator Hayes spoke. "Professor Okafor. Three billion dollars."

"Yes, Chair."

"For five years."

"Yes."

"Is that, in your view, achievable?"

"Chair, the Commonwealth is currently spending approximately $1.8 billion per annum on hyperscaler services. The total cost of the proposed programme is approximately the cost of one and a half years of current hyperscaler spend. The hyperscalers will continue to be necessary. The proposed programme is not a replacement. It is the cost of being able to negotiate."

Senator Daniel Patel, Greens, Victoria: "Professor, you used the word *custody.* That seems quite a strong word."

"It is a precise word, Senator. Custody is the legal and operational state of being held by another party. The data of the Australian Commonwealth, the workloads of Australian agencies, the case files of consular officers attending to Australian citizens in remand prisons overseas — these are, at this moment, in the custody of three American corporations operating under American law. They are not the property of those corporations. But they are, operationally, in their custody. And as today demonstrates, the custodian's other obligations can take precedence over the customer's needs without warning, without recourse, and without breach."

Senator Linda Cho, Labor, Western Australia: "Professor, the immediate question for many of my constituents is: when does it come back on?"

"Senator, I have spoken to Deputy Secretary Al-Farsi from DFAT this morning. She has been in contact with Washington. The honest answer is: we don't know. The Executive Order has no sunset. The compliance controls have no defined operational endpoint. The hyperscalers' capacity-management systems are operating as designed. The degradation is, in technical terms, a stable state. It can persist indefinitely without anyone making any further decision. The decisions that would end the degradation must be made by parties outside Australian jurisdiction."

Senator Hayes again: "Professor, thank you. The committee will reserve further questions until we have heard from the Deputy Secretary. We invite her to the table now."

Aisha Al-Farsi took David's place at the witness table. She had brought no notes. She poured herself water from the carafe. She waited.

Senator Hayes: "Deputy Secretary, thank you for attending. Can you walk us through where we stand diplomatically?"

"Chair, I'll be direct. The United States is a close ally and a long-standing partner. It is also a sovereign state acting in what its government understands as its national security interest. Those two facts are not in conflict, but they are in tension. My morning has been spent attempting to resolve that tension at the operational level. I have not yet succeeded.

"The Executive Order in question is, on its face, addressed to a specific competitive challenge — the management of advanced computing capacity in the context of strategic competition with the People's Republic of China. Australia is not a target. We are, however, what the State Department this morning called *unintended downstream impact.* I am told, in those exact words, that the United States deeply values the bilateral relationship and is very sorry. I am also told that it cannot provide a timeline.

"Diplomatically, I have three options open to me. The first is to escalate through the Quad — to seek to have the issue addressed in the broader strategic context with India and Japan, both of which I understand are experiencing similar degradation. I am pursuing this. The second is to negotiate a bilateral side-arrangement — a *CLOUD Act Executive Agreement* of the sort that was explored in 2019 — that would provide a structural mechanism for advance consultation in cases like this. I am pursuing this as well, but I should tell the committee that such an arrangement, even if concluded quickly, would not address the present degradation. It would only protect against the next one. The third option is more complex, and I will be candid: the only durable diplomatic response to a sovereignty issue is to be sovereign. Diplomacy can manage today. It cannot replace capability."

Senator Cho: "Deputy Secretary, what do you say to the Australian businesses, families, and citizens overseas who are being affected right now?"

Aisha did not answer for several seconds.

"Senator, I say to them: this is not your fault, and it is not your government's intent that you should be in this situation. It is, however, your government's responsibility. We made the procurement choices that led to this exposure. We will make different choices going forward. I cannot tell you when systems will be restored to normal. I can tell you that DFAT is operating manual fallback procedures for all consular cases — there is a team in the Casey Building working through them by hand, by phone, by fax where the receiving systems are willing to use it. If you or someone you love is in difficulty overseas, the embassy remains the embassy. We may be slower than usual. We are still here."

The hearing went for three hours. Senator Hayes ordered an interim report by 31 October — seventeen days. She instructed the secretariat to retain Professor Okafor and four colleagues of his choosing as standing technical advisers to the committee. She instructed the Department of Finance to commence the workload inventory David had recommended, with the first cut due by close of business Friday. By the time the committee rose, Aisha had taken three calls on her phone from her counterparts in Wellington, Tokyo, and Singapore, all of whom were experiencing variants of the same problem and all of whom wanted to talk.

David walked back to the ANU. He had not eaten since breakfast. He stopped at a sandwich shop in Civic and bought a chicken sandwich and ate it standing up on the footpath, watching the autumn leaves move across the lawn outside Old Parliament House. He thought about his father in Lagos, who had taught him that infrastructure was political even when politicians pretended it wasn't, and he thought about the citizens of the Commonwealth he had just spent three hours describing as being in someone else's custody, and he allowed himself one moment of unprofessional emotion before he wiped his mouth and went back to work.

### Industry — AWS Australia & New Zealand Headquarters, Sydney, Tuesday 4:30 PM

The General Manager of AWS Australia & New Zealand, an Australian named Patrick Lim who had spent fourteen years at Amazon before being promoted into the role in 2024, sat in a conference room with his executive team and tried to assemble a corporate position on an event he had not, in fact, caused. He had been in calls with Seattle since 5:00 AM Australia time, listening to the AWS global capacity management team explain, in calm and confident tones, that the workload reallocations being executed in response to EO 14118 were operating within the published terms of service, that capacity was being managed in accordance with applicable law in all jurisdictions, and that AWS Australia customers were not being singled out — they were merely experiencing, in the technical parlance, *secondary effects of compliance-driven capacity rebalancing.*

Patrick had asked, at three different points in three different calls, whether AWS could prioritise Commonwealth of Australia workloads on a temporary basis as a goodwill measure given the diplomatic sensitivities. The answer, each time, had been some variant of: *Patrick, you understand we can't preferentially treat any sovereign customer's workloads in a way that would expose us to allegations of arbitrary capacity allocation in other jurisdictions.* He understood. He also understood that the answer was *no.*

His head of public sector sales, a woman named Renata Costa who had been at AWS twelve years, said: "Patrick, we need to be honest with ourselves about what's about to happen. Our Australian government customer base is going to spend the next six months building exit strategies. Some of those will be performative. Some won't. The performative ones we can manage. The serious ones — the agencies that genuinely re-platform to Vault or AUCloud or sovereign Azure or some Frankenstein on-prem hybrid — we lose those for at least five years. Probably forever."

Patrick said: "I know."

"So what do we do?"

"We do three things. First, we double down on the position that distributed multi-region architecture is the best resilience strategy and that we are the platform best positioned to deliver it. That's defensible and mostly true. Second, we accelerate the sovereign cloud product roadmap that's been in PowerPoint hell since 2022. I want a real product, with real Australian-controlled key management and real contractual prioritisation guarantees, in market by Q3 2026. Third, we eat the loss on the agencies that are determined to leave. We do not fight them. We do not punish them at renewal. We help them migrate. Because if we punish them, the story becomes *AWS retaliated,* and the political consequences of that story are worse for us than losing the workloads."

Renata nodded. The head of comms, a former DFAT press secretary named Marcus Yi, said: "We should put Patrick on Insiders this Sunday. Apologise. Not for the EO — we didn't cause it — but for the experience. *Australian customers deserve better and we will do better.* Personal, contrite, forward-looking."

Patrick said: "Set it up."

Across town, in a much smaller office in Macquarie Park, the CEO of Vault Cloud — an Australian sovereign cloud provider that had been operating since 2018 and had spent seven years explaining patiently to procurement officers why the price premium over the hyperscalers was worth it — was watching her customer growth dashboard refresh and finding the experience surreal. Inbound enquiries that morning: 187. Normal Tuesday morning: 4. Her sales team was working off a list of Commonwealth agencies that had previously rejected Vault on price grounds. Every one of those agencies had called her by 3:00 PM. *The conversation we had in 2022,* she said to her CFO at 5:00 PM, *just became a completely different conversation.*

AUCloud — another Australian sovereign cloud provider, ASX-listed, smaller but with a defence-and-government focus — saw its share price rise 47 per cent on Tuesday and another 22 per cent on Wednesday. Its CEO went on the AFR podcast and spoke, with admirable restraint, about *being ready when the country needs sovereign capability.* He did not gloat. He did not need to.

The industry coalition Responsible Tech Australia — formed in the wake of the AllConnex incident eight months earlier — held an emergency call Tuesday evening. The hyperscaler members and the Australian-sovereign members had, until that day, been in a careful coalition of mutual non-aggression. By 9:00 PM the coalition was, in the careful internal language of its secretariat, *under stress.*

### Standards — ANU Centre for Responsible Technology, Tuesday 8:00 PM

David Okafor, his five standing-team agents, Aisha Al-Farsi, two of Aisha's team, and three people David had pulled in from the ANU School of Cybernetics sat around the conference table in CRT's Acton building and worked on what David's whiteboard already labelled, in his handwriting, *AS/NZS 9001:2026 — Cloud Sovereignty Assessment Framework.*

Lars Eriksson (DO-067), David's Technology Ethics Forecaster, ran the *Assume It Works Perfectly* exercise on every line of the draft. *If this framework works perfectly,* his question went, *what does it permit, what does it forbid, and what new failure modes does it create?* It was an excellent question and an annoying one, and David had based his entire research methodology on asking it before drafting anything.

The framework had four assessment dimensions.

*Legal jurisdiction* — under which sovereign authorities can the data and the workload be lawfully accessed, frozen, redirected, or compelled? The current Commonwealth posture treated this question as a privacy issue. The framework recommended treating it as a sovereignty issue.

*Operational control* — who, in operational practice, determines capacity allocation, deployment, configuration, and incident response? The framework distinguished between *contractual control* (what the customer is entitled to) and *practical control* (what the provider's automated systems actually do). EO 14118 had demonstrated that these two diverged.

*Substitution feasibility* — how quickly, and at what cost, could the workload be moved to an alternative provider or domestic infrastructure? The framework introduced a metric — *Sovereign Substitution Time* — measured in hours, days, weeks, or months. Most Commonwealth workloads, the team's preliminary assessment indicated, had a SST of *months* or *not feasible.*

*Continuity under adversarial conditions* — would the workload continue to operate if the provider were subject to a foreign regulatory direction adverse to Australian interests? This was the dimension EO 14118 had made unignorable.

Aisha contributed the diplomatic boundary conditions. The framework could not — must not — be drafted in a way that appeared to be anti-American. It had to be jurisdiction-neutral. The same assessment had to apply to a workload running in a Chinese, European, Indian, or Australian-domestic cloud. Sovereignty was a structural property, not a geopolitical alignment.

Chimamanda Obi (DO-022), David's Patent Strategy Developer, walked through the IP layer. Most sovereign cloud product offerings depended on technology licensed from the same hyperscalers they were meant to substitute for. Vault Cloud used VMware. AUCloud used Cisco. The Australian Government's *Hosting Certification Framework* certified facilities, not stacks. The framework's IP analysis would have to recommend, uncomfortably, a multi-year programme of building or acquiring substitution capability at the stack level, not just the facility level. *We can't have sovereign clouds running on stacks the hyperscalers can revoke,* Chimamanda said. *That's just custody with extra steps.*

By midnight the framework's first draft was eighteen pages. David called Standards Australia at 6:30 AM the next morning. Patricia Wong, the same chair David had worked with on AS/NZS 8700 after the AllConnex incident, took the call. *David, I assumed you'd be calling. We've already opened a working group slot. We can convene Thursday.*

By Friday — three days after the Senate inquiry opened — the working group had a draft public-comment text. Patricia would publish it the following Monday. Public comment period: 30 days, expedited. Mandatory adoption target: Q2 2026.

### Civil Society — National Press Club, Canberra, Wednesday 12:30 PM

Miguel Santos, executive director of the Australian Digital Rights Coalition, had not been planning to be in Canberra on Wednesday. He had been in Adelaide for a chapter launch. He had cancelled the launch at 7:00 AM Tuesday and flown overnight, arriving in Canberra at 4:00 AM, sleeping three hours at his sister's house in Belconnen, and walking into the National Press Club at 12:15 PM to deliver a speech he had drafted on the plane.

ADRC's official position on the Cloud Custody crisis had been published Tuesday afternoon, drafted by Miguel in a sandwich shop in Adelaide Airport and ratified by an emergency board call. It said three things. *First, the crisis is not a technical failure but a sovereignty failure, and Australians are the people who pay for it. Second, the Australian Digital Rights Coalition calls for the immediate repatriation of all Commonwealth-held citizen data to Australian-jurisdiction infrastructure with Australian-controlled key management — not as a long-term project but as a one-year programme. Third, no Australian citizen's data — particularly health, social services, taxation, or consular data — should ever again be in the operational custody of a non-Australian entity.*

Miguel delivered the speech to a packed room. He told three stories — Halima Yusuf at Bankstown Centrelink, Marcus Whitfield at his BAS portal in Northgate, and Tony Mitropoulos in a Thai remand cell. He named all three. He had spent the morning getting their permission, which Halima had given through her daughter, Marcus had given through his lawyer, and Tony's family had given through the Greek Orthodox priest who was their parish point of contact. He told the stories slowly, and he did not editorialise.

"These are not your customers," he said to the assembled press, the assembled bureaucrats, the assembled tech industry representatives in the back row. "These are not your users. These are the people the Commonwealth of Australia exists to serve. The pensioner. The small businessman. The young Australian in trouble overseas. They have been failed today by a system that was built for convenience and not for them. The Australian Digital Rights Coalition does not accept that this is the price of innovation. It is the price of a procurement decision made fifteen years ago and never revisited. It can be revisited. It must be revisited."

He sat down. The room was silent for three seconds and then, in a way that surprised everyone including Miguel, the room began to applaud — not just the journalists but the bureaucrats in the second row, the tech industry representatives in the back, the cleaning staff who had stopped by the door, the staffers from the Greens and Labor and the National Party and even one careful Liberal staffer in the corner.

The Indigenous data sovereignty groups — Maiam nayri Wingara, Lowitja Institute's data team, the National Centre of Indigenous Excellence — issued a joint statement that afternoon that the *AFR* reprinted in full on page seven. The statement opened: *We have been telling the Commonwealth for fifteen years that data sovereignty is not optional. That Indigenous community data, in particular, is not the property of the Commonwealth and cannot be in custody — Australian or foreign — without our consent. Today's crisis is the cost of not listening. The lesson is broader than today's incident: sovereignty is relational. Australia is in custody to the United States today; Indigenous communities have been in custody to Australia since 1788. The framework we need is one of negotiated sovereignty, not transferred custody.*

The statement was the most-shared civil society document of the week. By Friday it had been picked up by the *Guardian* (UK), *Le Monde,* and *The Hindu.*

### Public — Across Australia, Tuesday through Thursday

The public response to the Cloud Custody crisis was, in a way that took commentators several days to identify, more confused and slower-burning than the AllConnex response had been eight months earlier. AllConnex had locked people out of their houses. The houses had been visceral. The Cloud Custody crisis was, for most Australians, a series of *the website is slow today, isn't it* moments scattered across forty-seven services, none of which any individual citizen used all forty-seven of.

The aggregate, however, told a different story. By Wednesday morning, *Sydney Morning Herald* graphics showed: 1.8 million Australians had been unable to complete a MyGov transaction in the previous 36 hours; 340,000 had been unable to lodge a tax form; 47,000 had been unable to access Medicare claims; 8,200 small businesses were past or about to be past a regulatory deadline; an unknown number of overseas Australians had been unreachable by their embassies. The numbers were not as large as AllConnex. The implication was larger — these numbers were not consumer numbers. They were the operating numbers of the Australian state.

The Twitter/X response (which had survived its rebrand chaos and was, by 2025, somewhat stable again) trended #CloudCustody on Tuesday afternoon and stayed in the top ten Australian trends through Friday. The dominant tone was not the outrage that had characterised #SubDisconnect. It was, instead, a kind of grim recognition. Posts of the variant *I always thought we owned this stuff* and *who decided this?* and *why didn't anyone tell us?* recurred across demographics.

Reddit's r/australia had two top posts that week. The first, with 8,200 upvotes, was titled *Why is our entire federal government running on American servers and we never voted on this?* The top comment, 4,400 upvotes: *Because it was cheaper. Every procurement decision is cheaper. That's how you end up here. Cheaper is a strategy until it isn't.* The second post, 5,900 upvotes, linked to David Okafor's 2023 paper, which had been quietly sitting on the ANU CRT website with a couple of hundred downloads and which, by Friday, had over 280,000.

Indigenous communities that had been excluded from much of the digital economy's *convenience* for structural reasons found themselves, with bleak irony, somewhat insulated from the failure. Maiam nayri Wingara's statement to the Press Club — delivered in person by a softly-spoken senior researcher from Lowitja — included the line: *we have known for a long time that the digital infrastructure of this country was not built for us. The price of that exclusion is that we know how to live without it. This is not a recommendation. It is a fact, and the fact is uncomfortable for both of us.*

A teacher in Geelong whose Year 10 social studies class was that week studying the Westminster system asked the students to write 500 words on the question *what is sovereignty?* By Friday afternoon the assignment had been picked up — without the teacher's knowledge or consent — by the *Age,* which printed three of the responses on Saturday's opinion page. One, by a fifteen-year-old named Tahlia, opened: *Sovereignty is when you can do things without asking permission. We thought we were a country. It turns out we're a customer.*

The teacher kept the printout in her drawer for the rest of her career.
