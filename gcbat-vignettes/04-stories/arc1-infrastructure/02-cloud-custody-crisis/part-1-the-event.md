## Part 1 — The Event

### Halima Yusuf — 7:14 AM, Centrelink Service Centre, Bankstown

The queue had started forming at six. By seven it bent around the corner and down the block, past the chemist and the halal butcher and the laundromat with the *closed for renovation* sign that had been up since 2019. Halima Yusuf, sixty-eight years old, fled-Somalia-by-way-of-Kenya, had been in the queue since 6:42 AM. She was clutching a manila folder containing every document she could think of: Medicare card, driver's licence, lease agreement, three bank statements, a printout of her last pension payment, her certificate of citizenship dated 2003, and a passport-photo of her late husband because the case officer last year had asked for *next of kin verification* and she did not want to be sent away a second time.

The doors opened at 8:30 AM. The line moved.

By 9:15 Halima was sitting in front of a young man named Brendan, mid-twenties, lanyard, two screens. He typed. He waited. He typed again. He frowned.

"Mrs. Yusuf, I'm just having a bit of trouble bringing up your record. The system's running slow this morning."

"Slow?"

"Yeah, just give me a sec."

She waited. She had waited her whole life. Twelve years in a refugee camp outside Mombasa. Six months in detention in Port Hedland in 1995 before her status came through. Eighteen years cleaning offices in Western Sydney to qualify for citizenship and then for the pension she now relied on, since the cleaning had broken her back and her left hip. She knew how to wait.

But Brendan was getting nervous. He picked up the desk phone, dialled an extension. "Yeah, mate, Halima Yusuf, DOB 4/2/57, I can't pull her record up — system's giving me a 504. Yeah. Yeah, I tried that. Yeah." He hung up. "Mrs. Yusuf, I'm so sorry. There seems to be a problem with the database this morning. Can you come back tomorrow?"

"My pension was supposed to land yesterday. It did not land. I have rent on Friday."

Brendan looked at his screen, then at her, then at his screen again. He was a good kid. He had been at Centrelink eight months. He had not been trained for this.

"Mrs. Yusuf, I can see in the system that your payment was scheduled. I just can't see whether it processed. The — the part of the system that shows that is — it's not loading right now. If you come back tomorrow we should have it sorted."

"Tomorrow my rent will still be on Friday."

"I understand. I'm so sorry."

"What do I tell my landlord?"

"Mrs. Yusuf, I genuinely don't know what to tell you. Our systems are down. Everyone's are."

She stared at him. Then she stood up, very slowly, holding the back of the chair for balance. She had been here before. Different country, different decade, different excuse — *systems are down* was a new way of saying what governments had always said to people like her. *We cannot help you today.*

She walked out of the service centre. The queue still bent around the corner.

### Marcus Whitfield — 9:02 AM, Northgate Industrial Estate, Brisbane

Marcus Whitfield — not the GC-BAT Marcus, a different Marcus, the kind of name there are too many of in any given Australian story — owned three landscape supply yards across Brisbane's north. Forty-two employees. Annual revenue six point three million. He was forty-seven, had built the business from a single truck and a phone, and as of 9:02 AM Tuesday morning he was discovering that he could not lodge his Business Activity Statement.

The BAS was due tomorrow. He had left it to the last day because he always left it to the last day; that was his system, and it had worked for fourteen years. He had the figures from his bookkeeper. He had logged into the ATO portal at 8:47 AM. The page had loaded slowly. He had filled out the figures. He had clicked Submit.

*Sorry, there's been a problem. Please try again later.*

He clicked Submit again.

*Sorry, there's been a problem. Please try again later.*

He refreshed the page. He logged out. He logged back in. The login itself took ninety seconds. He filled the figures again. He clicked Submit.

*Sorry, there's been a problem. Please try again later.*

By 9:30 AM Marcus had called his bookkeeper, who told him she was hearing the same thing from three other clients. He called his accountant. Same story. He called the ATO helpline. The recorded message thanked him for his call and informed him that wait times were currently in excess of four hours due to high call volume.

He sat in his office, looking at the *Sorry* page, and did the maths. Late BAS lodgement penalty: $313 per period. Failure-to-pay penalty: interest at the General Interest Charge rate, currently around 11.15% per annum, applied daily, on a GST liability of $84,000. If this dragged on a week, he was looking at three hundred dollars plus daily interest of about twenty-five dollars. Annoying. If it dragged on a month, he was looking at four figures in penalties for a problem that was not his. He had clicked Submit. He had the screenshot to prove it. The screenshot would not matter to anyone.

He called the ATO again. The recorded message thanked him for his call.

### David Okafor — 6:14 AM, ANU Centre for Responsible Technology, Canberra

The email had arrived at 11:47 PM the previous night. David Okafor read it at 6:14 AM, sitting at the kitchen table of his Forrest townhouse with his coffee cooling beside him, and felt the particular feeling of being a Type Six who has just been proven right and finds no satisfaction in it whatsoever.

The email was from Hassan Al-Rashid, his Academic Research Liaison, agent ID DO-033. Hassan worked Adelaide time, which was Adelaide time, but the underlying message was from a Senate Estimates committee secretariat in Canberra:

> **From:** Senate Standing Committees on Foreign Affairs, Defence and Trade
> **Subject:** URGENT — Witness request, Cloud Services Sovereignty Inquiry
> **To:** Prof. David Okafor, ANU CRT
>
> The Committee has convened an emergency inquiry into the ongoing disruption to Commonwealth cloud-hosted services. We require your testimony as the author of *Cloud Sovereignty as Foreign Policy Risk: When Critical Data Lives Abroad* (CRT Working Paper 2023-14). First sitting: today, 14 October 2025, 14:00 AEDT. Pre-circulated brief required by 11:00 AM. Please confirm attendance.

David read it twice. He pulled up the news feeds his standing-agent team curated for him each morning. The pattern was already visible if you knew what to look for. Centrelink mygov outages reported overnight on social media — scattered, dismissed as routine. ATO portal "experiencing intermittent issues." DFAT Smartraveller updates not loading. Medicare Online Claims portal showing 504 errors. AWS Australia status page: *all systems operational.* Microsoft Azure Australia status page: *all systems operational.* Google Cloud Australia status page: *all systems operational.*

All systems operational and yet nothing was working.

He opened a second browser, navigated to the US Department of Commerce Bureau of Industry and Security feed. There it was — Executive Order 14118, signed Sunday US time, Monday Australia time. *Expansion of Entity List restrictions on advanced computing technology and associated services.* Reading between the lines, the EO required US hyperscalers to immediately implement enhanced compliance controls on workloads originating from or processing data related to a long list of foreign entities. The list included, among many others, a number of Chinese cloud regions. The compliance controls required real-time inspection, geographic redistribution of certain workloads, and — buried on page seventeen of the technical addendum — *throttling and prioritisation of remaining capacity to ensure continuity of US national security workloads.*

Australia was not on the Entity List. Australia was not the target. Australia was, however, hosting most of its Commonwealth government workloads on AWS Sydney, Azure Australia East, and Google Cloud Sydney — and those regions shared physical and logical capacity with regions that had just been told to drop everything and re-architect.

It was not a failure. It was a deprioritisation. Australia had become, overnight, a tier-two customer of its own information infrastructure.

David stood up, walked to his study, took down the printed copy of his 2023 paper from the shelf, and turned to page 34, where he had drawn a diagram with the caption: *Failure mode 4 — silent capacity reallocation under foreign regulatory pressure.* He had written: *This failure mode is the most insidious because no actor breaches any contract or violates any law. The hyperscaler complies with US law as required; the Australian customer experiences degradation as a normal symptom of cloud elasticity; the constitutional question of where sovereignty resides remains unasked.*

He had been thirty-eight when he wrote that. He was now forty, and the unasked question was being asked by a Senate inquiry at fourteen-hundred hours, and his coffee was cold.

He called Hassan. "Hassan. Wake everyone. I need the team in by eight. Tell Chimamanda to pull the IP licensing analysis from the 2024 update. Tell Lars to dust off the *Assume It Works Perfectly* run we did on the AWS reallocation scenario — I think that was Q3 of last year. Tell Priya Rao I need the methodology section of the original paper re-validated against what's actually happening this morning. Tell Isabella I need three scenarios for Senate by noon — best case, expected, worst case."

"Already on it, professor. I sent the meeting invite at 4:30 this morning."

"Of course you did. Thank you, Hassan."

"Professor — there's something else."

"Go."

"DFAT called the office at 5:15. They want you to brief their Deputy Secretary for Digital Diplomacy before the Senate session. She's coming in person at 11:00."

"Aisha Al-Farsi?"

"Yes."

David exhaled slowly. He had met Aisha twice. Once at a Lowy Institute panel in 2022 on digital sovereignty in the Indo-Pacific, where they had disagreed politely about whether sovereignty could be technical or had to be political. Once at a Commonwealth dinner where she had made him tea and they had ended up talking for forty minutes about Lagos and Muscat and how port cities raise their children differently from inland cities. He had liked her. He had also known, watching her work the room afterwards, that she was the most dangerous person at the dinner — not because she sought power, but because she did not, which meant she had access to all of it.

"Tell her welcome," David said. "I'll have the brief ready."

He hung up. He walked back to the kitchen, poured the cold coffee down the sink, and made a fresh pot. The kettle worked. The kettle did not depend on AWS Sydney. He noted this with something like gratitude.

### Aisha Al-Farsi — 5:47 AM, DFAT R.G. Casey Building, Canberra

Aisha Al-Farsi had been on the phone with Washington since 5:47 AM. Or — more accurately — she had been on the phone with a Special Assistant in the US State Department Bureau of Cyberspace and Digital Policy who was working very hard to convey, in the diplomatic vocabulary of phrases like *we deeply value the bilateral relationship* and *unintended downstream impacts,* that the United States had not, in any way, intended for Australia to be inconvenienced by Executive Order 14118 and was very sorry to hear that Australia was experiencing — what was the term used? — *transient service variability.*

"Transient," Aisha said, in her quiet voice that did not need to be raised because she was incapable of being interrupted, "is a word that suggests a return to baseline. Can you assist me with the timeline for return to baseline?"

The Special Assistant could not, presently, assist her with the timeline.

"I see," Aisha said. "And the compliance controls — are they being implemented uniformly across all US hyperscaler regions, or specifically against workloads associated with the designated entities?"

The Special Assistant believed they were being implemented in accordance with the EO's stated objectives.

"I understand. So if I have a Centrelink workload running in AWS Sydney that has no relationship whatsoever to any designated entity, that workload should not be experiencing degradation, correct?"

The Special Assistant noted that the operational details of hyperscaler compliance implementation were a matter for the providers and that the EO did not direct specific operational responses.

"I see," Aisha said. She allowed the silence to last for four seconds. Then: "I'm going to need to convey to my Minister that the United States cannot, at this time, provide either a timeline for restoration or a confirmation that the affected workloads are within the EO's intended scope. Is that an accurate summary of where we are?"

A pause on the other end. Then a careful: "Madam Deputy Secretary, we are working to clarify these matters and will provide updates as they become available."

"Thank you. I will look forward to your update."

Aisha hung up. She made tea — the small electric kettle on the credenza in her office, the Omani green tea she kept in a tin from a vendor in the Mutrah souk her father had introduced her to thirty years ago — and she sat looking at the harbour view that was not Sydney Harbour because she worked in Canberra and the harbour view was a print on the wall, and she allowed herself sixty seconds of feeling exactly what she felt, which was a controlled and useful kind of anger.

Then she opened her laptop and began drafting talking points for the Foreign Minister.

She knew the law. She had read the Cloud Act. She had read the recent BIS regulations. The Americans were not wrong, technically. They were exercising sovereign authority over their own corporations and infrastructure. The Australian government had voluntarily made itself dependent on that infrastructure. The treaty framework — the so-called *CLOUD Act Executive Agreement* the previous Australian government had explored in 2019, then quietly shelved — would have provided a mechanism for advance consultation. Without it, Australia was a customer, not a partner. Customers got served when the provider's other obligations permitted.

This was not, Aisha thought, a failure of the Americans. It was a failure of every Australian government, going back to 2010, that had chosen the convenience of foreign hyperscaler services over the discipline of sovereign capability. The hyperscalers had built the better product. Australia had bought it. Now Australia was discovering what *bought* meant.

She finished her tea. She read David Okafor's 2023 paper for the third time since she had downloaded it overnight. She underlined two sentences from the conclusion:

*Sovereignty is not a property of laws or borders. It is a property of capability. A nation that cannot independently operate the systems on which its government depends has, to that extent, ceded sovereignty.*

She wrote in the margin: *David — agreed. Now what?*

She put the paper in her satchel. She walked out of her office, down the corridor, to the lift that would take her to her car that would drive her across Canberra to the ANU Centre for Responsible Technology, where she would arrive at 11:00 AM and David Okafor would have made her, she suspected, tea — possibly not very good tea, because he was Nigerian, not Omani, but tea nonetheless — and they would begin the work of explaining to a Senate committee why a foreign executive order had degraded the operations of the Australian Commonwealth, and what to do about it.

The lift descended. Aisha Al-Farsi, who was capable of carrying enormous amounts of conflict without showing it, allowed her shoulders to drop one centimetre and then resumed her bearing.

It was going to be a long week.

### Tony Mitropoulos — 11:47 AM Bangkok local time, Bang Kwang Remand Prison, Nonthaburi

Tony Mitropoulos was twenty-three years old, from Footscray in Melbourne, on his first solo overseas trip, and he had been in a Thai remand prison for nineteen hours. He had been arrested at Suvarnabhumi Airport on his way home after his cousin in Krabi had handed him a souvenir cigarette tin that had, it turned out, contained more than tobacco. Tony believed his cousin. The Thai police did not, presently, care what Tony believed.

He had been allowed one phone call. He had called the Australian embassy in Bangkok. The line had not connected. He had been told he could try again later. *Later* in a Thai remand prison meant when the guard remembered.

It was now eleven forty-seven AM. He had been awake for thirty-one hours. He was sitting on a concrete floor with eight other foreign men, two of whom spoke English. One was a French citizen who had been there four days; the other was a Dutch man who had been there nine days and had stopped speaking in coherent sentences. Tony had not eaten since the flight. He could not remember if he had drunk water since the arrest.

At the Australian embassy in Bangkok, three kilometres away, a consular officer named Beth Tran was sitting at her desk in front of a screen that should have been displaying Tony's case file — which the Bangkok police had transmitted to DFAT's consular case management system overnight via the secure connection that all 110 Australian missions overseas used — and was instead displaying a small spinning circle and the words *Loading...* The consular case management system was hosted in the Microsoft Azure Australia East region in Sydney. The Australia East region was experiencing, per Microsoft's status page, *all systems operational.* Beth's screen had been displaying *Loading...* for two hours and forty minutes.

She had a printed contact list from 2019 in her drawer. She used it. She called Bangkok Remand Centre directly. She told them, in her serviceable Thai, that the Australian Government would like to send a consular officer to interview an Australian citizen named Anthony Mitropoulos, DOB 14 March 2002. The remand centre asked for the case file reference number. Beth did not have it. The case file was in the system. The system was *Loading...*

The remand centre's duty officer, who had been on shift for sixteen hours and had his own problems, told Beth that without the reference number a visit could not be arranged. Beth offered to fax the case detail. The remand centre's fax machine was, the duty officer informed her, broken since June. Beth offered to come in person with a printed declaration. The duty officer, more politely than was strictly required, said she was welcome to come in person, but without the reference number she would not be permitted past the visitor's gate. He suggested she call back when her systems were working.

Beth Tran sat in her office. She wrote on a yellow legal pad: *Mitropoulos, A. — first sighting overdue. Welfare unknown. Cannot access case file. Cannot reach Canberra duty officer (DFAT internal Teams down). Will try again in 30 min.*

Tony Mitropoulos, on the concrete floor of the remand cell, leaned his head against the wall. He had stopped crying about three hours ago. He thought about his mother, who would not yet know. He thought about the embassy, which he had been told all his life would be there if he ever got into trouble overseas. He wondered, in a way that was not yet panic but was on its way to becoming panic, why no one had come.

The Microsoft Azure Australia East region remained *all systems operational.* The DFAT consular case management system, which used six microservices distributed across three availability zones in that region, was waiting for two of those microservices to respond to health checks that had, as of approximately 4:30 AM AEDT, begun returning HTTP 504 Gateway Timeout because the underlying compute capacity allocated to those services had been silently re-prioritised by an automated capacity-management system in Redmond, Washington, in compliance with an Executive Order signed by the President of the United States the previous Sunday.

Tony Mitropoulos was not on any list. Tony Mitropoulos had not breached any export control. Tony Mitropoulos was a twenty-three-year-old from Footscray who had trusted an embassy, and the embassy had been built on infrastructure that was, at this moment, serving someone else's national security.
